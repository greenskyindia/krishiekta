CTT on essential food items unwarranted

\n\nG. Chandrashekhar\n

\n\nMumbai, June 20:  \n\n
\n

Pursuant to the Finance Minister’s last budget speech proposing that commodity transaction tax (CTT) will be levied on futures contract transactions at the commodities futures exchanges and that agri-commodities will be exempt from such levy, the ministry has notified a list of 23 commodities that will be exempt from CTT. All other commodities covering energy products, metals and so on will be subject to CTT.

The Ministry, in its wisdom, has endeavoured to restrict the list of exempted commodities and it is understandable, given the revenue implication. But the bigger question is whether the notification dated June 19 actually reflects and indeed advances what the FM clearly stated in his Budget speech or whether there is a deviation between intent and execution.

What did the FM say on February 28? He said, CTT will be levied on non-agricultural commodities futures contracts at the same rate as on equity futures that is at 0.01 per cent of the price of the trade. He repeated that agricultural commodities will be exempt.

Also, one cannot overlook what the FM said in the early part of his speech.

“Looming large over our efforts to stimulate growth is inflation. . . . . It is food inflation that is worrying and we shall take all possible steps to augment the supply side to meet the growing demand for food items.”

Does the Department of Revenue notification advance what the FM observed or does it run counter to it? It is clear, the Ministry has sought to include in the exempted category agri-commodities that can be called ‘primary agricultural commodities’ that is those that have not undergone any significant processing.

But the inclusion of two items in the exempted category – cottonseed oilcake and soyameal – which are essentially processed commodities and are by-products of oilseed crushing raises suspicion. The intent could not have been to restrict the exempted list to primary agri-commodities as the two items are at least one step removed from the primary agri-commodity that is cottonseed and soyabean.

If cottonseed cake and soyameal which are not strictly primary agri-commodities can be exempted from CTT for whatever reason, there is no reason why other essentially agricultural commodities processed in some form or other – various vegetable oils, sugar, guar gum, for instance – should be subjected to the tax.

The Revenue Department’s decision to tax futures trade in essential food commodities beats logic. Taxing futures transactions of essential foods such as vegetable oil and sugar is unwarranted. It is unlikely serve any purpose except raise the cost of hedging and thereby discourage hedgers. If anything, CTT on commodities such as vegetable oil and sugar may prove counter-productive. These commodities have a high weightage in consumer price index. Why tax them, even at a very modest rate?

Importantly, taxing the transactions of essential commodities such as sugar and vegetable oil is sure to defeat the objective of the government to enhance hedger participation in the market. The Ministry of Consumer Affairs, the market regulator Forward Markets Commission and commodity futures exchanges are all the while engaged in educating hedgers and potential hedgers about risk management. The effort is likely to be frustrated. Obviously, one arm of the Government (Department of Revenue) does not know what the other arm (Department of Consumer Affairs) is doing.

Also, it is unclear if business rivalry played any part in drawing the list of exempted commodities.

There is a compelling case for expanding the list of exempted agricultural commodities by including sugar, various edible oil and similar items that are products made out of primary agri-commodities. Even in trade parlance, edible oil and sugar are recognised as agri-commodities.

\n(This article was published on June 22, 2013)
